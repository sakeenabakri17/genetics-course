🔬Introduction to Epigenetics

The term epigenetics was coined in 1942 by British developmental biologist Conrad Waddington — combining the Greek prefix "epi" (above/over) with "genetics." Epigenetics describes regulatory mechanisms that act upon the genome without altering the underlying DNA sequence.

Core Definition

Epigenetics = Regulation of gene expression through chromatin chemical modifications without actual change in the gene sequence. Encompasses collective, heritable changes in phenotype arising independent of the primary DNA sequence.

Gene Count Paradox
  • Homo sapiens: ~19,962 genes; genome 3.2 Gb
  • C. elegans: ~19,984 genes; genome 100.3 Mb
  • Zea mays (maize): ~42,580 genes; genome 2.4 Gb
  • Differences in complexity = gene regulation, not gene number
Classic Experiments
  • Dutch Famine (1944): Malnourished pregnant women → offspring with increased CVD, diabetes decades later — transgenerational epigenetic inheritance
  • Agouti Mice (Jirtle & Dolinoy, 2003): Methylated Agouti gene → brown healthy mice; unmethylated → yellow obese mice. Maternal diet (folic acid, choline) determined offspring phenotype.

🧪Epigenetic Mechanism 1: DNA Methylation

Transfer of methyl group from SAM (S-adenosylmethionine) to the 5-position of cytosines in CpG dinucleotides → producing 5-methylcytosine (5-mC). SAM availability links cellular metabolism to the epigenome.

Key Enzymes: DNA Methyltransferases (DNMTs)
DNMT1 — Maintenance Methylase

Methylates hemimethylated CpG sites during replication. Ensures faithful transmission of methylation patterns to daughter cells. Provides the mechanism for epigenetic memory via symmetric CpG positioning.

DNMT3A & DNMT3B — De novo Methylases

Establish new methylation at previously unmethylated positions. Critical during embryogenesis and differentiation. DNMT3A mutations found in ~20% of AML (commonly R882H dominant-negative).

Mechanisms of Methylation-Induced Gene Silencing
  • Direct blocking: Methylated cytosines physically prevent transcription factor binding.
  • Indirect — MBP pathway: Methyl-CpG binding proteins (MBPs, e.g., MeCP2) recruit HDAC complexes → histone deacetylation → chromatin compaction → transcription inaccessible.
⚠️ No Direct Demethylase

There is NO direct DNA demethylase enzyme. Demethylation is oxidative — via TET (Ten-Eleven Translocation) dioxygenases:

1
TET Oxidation

TET1/2/3 convert 5-mC → 5-hydroxymethylcytosine (5-hmC)

2
Further Oxidation

5-hmC → 5-formylcytosine (5-fC) → 5-carboxycytosine (5-caC)

3
Base Excision Repair

5-caC excised by TDG glycosylase → BER → unmethylated C restored

🏥 Clinical: Epigenetic Drugs

HDAC inhibitors: Vorinostat (SAHA) + Romidepsin → FDA-approved for T-cell lymphoma. DNMT inhibitors: 5-Azacytidine (Vidaza) → FDA-approved for MDS/AML. EZH2 inhibitor: Tazemetostat → follicular lymphoma.

🔭Analytical Methods: ChIP-Seq & Bisulfite Sequencing

Bisulfite Sequencing — DNA Methylation Mapping
1
Bisulfite Treatment

Single-stranded DNA + NaHSO₃: Unmethylated C → Uracil (U); 5-methylcytosine UNCHANGED

2
PCR Amplification

U reads as Thymine (T). So: T = was unmethylated C; C = was methylated

3
Sequencing & Analysis

Compare with reference genome → single-CpG resolution methylation map

ChIP-Seq — Chromatin Immunoprecipitation + Sequencing
1
Cross-linking

1% Formaldehyde (RT 15 min) → covalently links proteins to DNA; quenched with glycine

2
Fragmentation

Cell lysis + SDS. Sonication → ~200 bp chromatin fragments

3
Immunoprecipitation

Target-specific antibody (e.g., anti-H3K4me3) + protein A/G beads → capture antibody-protein-DNA complexes

4
Reverse Cross-linking

Elution + Proteinase K → recover DNA

5
Library & Sequencing

Illumina adaptors → PCR → high-throughput sequencing → bioinformatic peak calling (MACS2)

🧬The Central Dogma & Key Definitions

Central Dogma

DNA → RNA → Protein. DNA stores and transmits hereditary information. RNA is the intermediary. Proteins carry out cellular functions.

  • Human genome: 3.2 Gb
  • Standard amino acids: 20

  • Eukaryotic ribosome: 80S (60S + 40S)

🔗DNA Replication

Semi-conservative Replication

Each daughter dsDNA = one old strand + one new strand (Meselson-Stahl experiment, 1958). Replication is bidirectional from origins. Only 5′→3′ synthesis: leading strand continuous; lagging strand discontinuous (Okazaki fragments ~100-200 nt in eukaryotes).

📝Transcription: RNA Pol II & the Initiation Complex

RNA PolProductsLocation
RNA Pol I45S pre-rRNA → 28S, 18S, 5.8SNucleolus
RNA Pol IIAll mRNA + snRNAs + miRNA pri-transcriptsNucleus (euchromatin)
RNA Pol IIItRNA, 5S rRNA, U6 snRNANucleus

TATA box (~-30 bp from TSS) → TFIID/TBP binds → PIC assembly → TFIIH/CDK7 phosphorylates Ser-5 of CTD → initiation. Pol II pauses ~30-60 nt downstream. P-TEFb/CDK9 phosphorylates Ser-2 of CTD → releases pause → productive elongation.

CTD Phosphorylation Code

Ser-5-P (CDK7/TFIIH): initiation + capping enzyme recruitment. Ser-2-P (P-TEFb/CDK9): pause release + elongation + splicing/polyadenylation machinery recruitment.

🔧Post-transcriptional Processing

5′ Capping
1
RNA Triphosphatase

Removes γ-phosphate from 5′ end: 5′-pppN → 5′-ppN

2
Guanylyl Transferase

Adds GMP → 5′→5′ triphosphate linkage (GpppN) — unusual!

3
7-Methyltransferase (+ SAM)

Methylates N7 of guanine → m7GpppN (the 5′ cap)

Splicing

Spliceosome (U1, U2, U4, U5, U6 snRNPs): U1 binds 5′ GU → U2 binds branch point A → U4/U6/U5 joins → U1+U4 destabilized → lariat formation → exon ligation. ~95% of human multi-exon genes undergo alternative splicing.

Poly-A Tailing

AAUAAA poly-A signal → CPSF + CstF → CPSF73 cleaves 10-30 nt downstream → PAP adds 50-250 adenosines → mRNA stability, export, translation.

🏥 Clinical Applications

DMD exon skipping: Eteplirsen/PMOs skip exon 51 → converts out-of-frame to in-frame → partial dystrophin. SMA — Nusinersen (Spinraza): ASO blocks ISS-N1 in SMN2 intron 7 → exon 7 included → full-length SMN protein.

🧬Mitochondrial DNA

mtDNA Key Facts

Circular, double-stranded, 16,569 bp. Encodes: 13 proteins (all ETC/ATP synthase subunits) + 22 tRNAs + 2 rRNAs. Maternal inheritance. High mutation rate (~10-17× nuclear DNA). Mutation rate exploited in forensics and population genetics.

Inheritance & Heteroplasmy
  • Maternal inheritance: Sperm mtDNA eliminated post-fertilization
  • Homoplasmy: All mtDNA identical (mutant or WT)
  • Heteroplasmy: Mixture of mutant + WT mtDNA
  • Threshold effect: Disease when mutant exceeds ~60-90%
Mitochondrial Genetic Code Differences
  • UGA = Tryptophan (not stop codon)
  • AUA = Methionine (not Isoleucine)
  • AGA/AGG = Stop codons (not Arginine)
  • Only 22 tRNAs needed (wobble rules differ)

🔬The Non-coding RNA World

Historical Milestone

In 1993, Rosalind Lee et al. and Gary Ruvkun et al. independently established miRNAs (lin-4 in C. elegans) — paradigm-shifting discovery. HGP (2003) revealed only ~3% of human genome encodes proteins; the vast majority is transcribed as non-coding RNAs.

🎯miRNA: Biogenesis & Mechanism

1
Transcription (Nucleus)

RNA Pol II transcribes primary miRNA (pri-miRNA) — has characteristic hairpin structure

2
Drosha Processing (Nucleus)

Microprocessor complex (Drosha RNase III + DGCR8 cofactor) cleaves pri-miRNA ~11 bp from hairpin base → ~70 nt pre-miRNA

3
Nuclear Export

Exportin-5 + RanGTP exports pre-miRNA to cytoplasm

4
Dicer Processing

Dicer (RNase III) cleaves pre-miRNA → ~22 bp miRNA duplex

5
RISC Loading

Guide strand (less stable 5′ end — asymmetry rule) loaded into RISC with Argonaute (AGO) protein

6
Target Silencing

miRNA directs RISC to 3′ UTR of target mRNAs via seed sequence (nt 2-8) base-pairing → translational repression or mRNA degradation

🏥 Clinical miRNA

miR-15a/16 deletion (13q14) in CLL → BCL2 overexpression → venetoclax target. miR-21 oncomiR (overexpressed in 20+ cancers) → silences PTEN, PDCD4. Inclisiran (GalNAc-siRNA targeting PCSK9 mRNA, FDA 2021) → twice-yearly injection for LDL reduction.

✂️siRNA & piRNA

siRNA — Small Interfering RNA
  • ~20-25 bp dsRNA processed by Dicer
  • Loaded into RISC with AGO2 (slicer activity)
  • Perfect complementarity → AGO2 cleaves mRNA between positions 10-11
  • Highly specific for ONE target (vs miRNA = broad)
  • Natural role: defense against transposons & RNA viruses
  • Therapeutics: inclisiran, patisiran, givosiran
piRNA — PIWI-Interacting RNA
  • 24-31 nt; longer than miRNA/siRNA
  • Associates with PIWI proteins (germline Argonaute)
  • Highly abundant in testes and oocytes
  • Self-amplified by Ping-Pong cycle
  • Primary function: transposon silencing in germline — genome integrity protection
  • Dicer-independent processing

✏️Genome Editing & CRISPR History

CRISPR Acronym

Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats. Found in bacteria/archaea as adaptive immune memory. First described in E. coli (Ishino, 1987). Biological function established by Mojica & Barrangou (2005-2007). Cas9 biochemistry by Doudna & Charpentier (Science, 2012) → Nobel Prize 2020.

Previous genome editing tools (ZFNs, TALENs) used protein-DNA binding modules fused to FokI nuclease — difficult to synthesize, expensive, hard to multiplex. CRISPR uses RNA-guided DNA targeting → programmable by simply changing 20 nt of the sgRNA spacer sequence.

🦠Bacterial Adaptive Immune System

1
Adaptation (Phage Infection)

Cas1 + Cas2 cut ~20-26 nt protospacer from viral DNA immediately upstream of NGG PAM → integrated between CRISPR repeats in bacterial chromosome

2
Expression — crRNA Biogenesis

RNA Pol transcribes CRISPR array → pre-crRNA. tracrRNA base-pairs with repeats. RNase III cleaves → individual crRNA:tracrRNA duplexes

3
Cas9 Loading

crRNA:tracrRNA duplex associates with Cas9 protein → surveillance complex

4
Interference — Target Cleavage

Cas9 scans DNA for NGG PAM → unwinds DNA → crRNA base-pairs with viral sequence (R-loop) → HNH + RuvC cleave both strands → virus destroyed

Cas9 DomainFunction
PAM-Interacting (PI)Recognizes NGG PAM — determines target specificity
HNHCleaves the strand COMPLEMENTARY to crRNA (target strand)
RuvCCleaves the NON-complementary strand
dCas9 (D10A+H840A)No nuclease activity — used for CRISPRi/CRISPRa/epigenome editing

🧫CRISPR-Cas9 in the Laboratory

In the lab, the natural two-RNA system simplified to a single sgRNA — only 20 nt spacer needs changing to target any gene near NGG PAM (occurs ~every 8 bp in the human genome).

Plasmid Transfection (Temporary, 3-7 days)
  1. Purchase sgRNA plasmid (glycerol stock)
  2. Propagate in LB + antibiotic → purify plasmid
  3. Mix with transfection reagent (Axol-ReadyFect) → lipoplexes → add to cells
  4. Assay modification after 24-72 hr
Lentiviral Transduction (Permanent)
  1. Transfect HEK293T with sgRNA plasmid + VSV-G env + gag-pol plasmids
  2. Harvest virus from media (48-72 hr)
  3. Infect target cells with viral supernatant
  4. Select with Puromycin → pure modified population
DNA Repair Outcomes After Cas9 Cleavage
NHEJ — Gene Knockout
  • Active throughout ALL cell cycle phases
  • Error-prone → small indels → frameshift → premature stop → gene KO
  • Proteins: Ku70/80, DNA-PKcs, Artemis, XRCC4, DNA Lig IV, XLF
  • Standard approach for loss-of-function studies
HDR — Precise Editing
  • Active mainly in S/G2 phase only
  • Requires homologous donor template (ssODN or plasmid)
  • Precise sequence correction/insertion possible
  • Less efficient than NHEJ — major barrier for therapy
  • Used for: gene correction (SCD, β-thalassemia)

💊Therapeutic Applications & Ethics

🏥 FDA-Approved CRISPR Therapy

Casgevy (exa-cel, Vertex/CRISPR Therapeutics, Dec 2023): First FDA-approved CRISPR therapy. Disrupts BCL11A erythroid enhancer in autologous HSCs → HbF reactivation → treats sickle cell disease and β-thalassemia. Other advances: CRISPR-edited CAR-T cells; base editing (CBE: C→T; ABE: A→G); prime editing (any point mutation without DSB).

⚠️ He Jiankuis 2018 Germline Editing Controversy

Edited CCR5 in human embryos for HIV resistance. Universally condemned: (1) No medical necessity, (2) Unknown off-target effects, (3) No regulatory approval, (4) Heritable germline changes affecting future generations. Sentenced to 3 years imprisonment in China. Led to international governance framework for germline editing.

TFIIA facilitates TFIID/TBP binding at TATA box (~-30 bp from TSS). TBP bends DNA ~90°.

2

TFIID recognized by TFIIB → recruits RNA Pol II with TFIIF

Phosphorylates Ser-5 of CTD heptad (YSPTSPS) → transcription initiation + capping enzyme recruitment

CTD Phosphorylation Code

Ser-5-P (CDK7): initiation mark + recruits capping enzymes + H3K4me3 methyltransferases. Ser-2-P (P-TEFb/CDK9): elongation mark + recruits splicing/polyadenylation machinery. P-TEFb activated by BRD4 reading H3K27ac at enhancers → links enhancer activity to elongation.

🔧Post-transcriptional Processing: Capping, Splicing, Poly-A

5′ Capping
1
RNA Triphosphatase

Removes γ-phosphate from 5′ end: 5′-pppN → 5′-ppN

2
Guanylyl Transferase

Adds GMP → unusual 5′→5′ triphosphate linkage (GpppN) — resistant to 5′ exonucleases

3
7-Methyltransferase (+SAM)

Methylates N7 of guanine → m7GpppN cap. Functions: exonuclease protection, eIF4E recognition, nuclear export signal

Splicing

Spliceosome: U1 binds 5′ GU → U2 binds branch point A → U4/U6/U5 tri-snRNP → lariat formation → exon ligation. Alternative splicing types: exon skipping, intron retention, alternative 5′/3′ splice sites, mutually exclusive exons. Regulated by SR proteins (activate ESEs) and hnRNPs (repress ESSs).

🏥 Splicing in Medicine

Nusinersen (Spinraza, SMA): ASO blocks ISS-N1 in SMN2 intron 7 → exon 7 inclusion → full-length SMN protein (FDA 2016). Exon skipping in DMD: Eteplirsen (Exondys 51) converts out-of-frame deletion to in-frame → partial dystrophin. BRCA1 silent mutation: c.4308T>C disrupts ESE → exon 11 skipping → truncated BRCA1 → hereditary breast cancer risk.

🚂mRNA Transport & Translation Regulation

Poly-A Tailing: AAUAAA poly-A signal → CPSF + CstF → CPSF73 cleaves 10-30 nt downstream → PAP adds 50-250 adenosines → mRNA stability + export + translation (PABP interaction).

Nuclear Export: NXF1-NXT1 (TAP-p15) complex guides mRNA-RNP through NPC. DDX19 helicase removes export factors in cytoplasm.

Translation Initiation: eIF4E binds m7G cap → eIF4F complex (eIF4E+eIF4G+eIF4A) + 43S PIC (40S+eIF2-GTP-Met-tRNAi) → scan for AUG → 60S joins. mTORC1 promotes translation by phosphorylating 4E-BP1 (releases eIF4E) and S6K1.

Stress Granules & P-bodies

Under stress: eIF2α phosphorylation → global translation inhibition → stalled mRNAs condense into stress granules (G3BP1, TIA-1) via LLPS. P-bodies contain mRNA decay machinery (DCP1/2, CCR4-NOT, XRN1). mRNAs can be stored in SGs/P-bodies and re-released when stress resolves.

🛡️mRNA Quality Control: NMD & Alternative Splicing

Nonsense-Mediated Decay (NMD)

Triggered when PTC is >50-55 nt upstream of the last exon-exon junction (EJC mark) → UPF1 bridges eRF complex to downstream EJC → SMG1 phosphorylates UPF1 → SMG6 endonuclease + XRN1 + exosome degrade mRNA. Protects against dominant-negative truncated proteins. Also regulates ~5-10% of normal transcriptome.

m6A Epitranscriptomics

m6A (N6-methyladenosine) is the most abundant internal mRNA modification. Writers: METTL3-METTL14-WTAP complex at DRACH motifs. Erasers: FTO, ALKBH5. Readers: YTHDF1 (promotes translation), YTHDF2 (promotes degradation), YTHDF3 (both), YTHDC1 (nuclear export + splicing). Dysregulated in leukemia, GBM, and other cancers.

🌐Reductionism vs Systems Biology

Reductionism (Classical)
  • Isolates and studies individual components (genes, proteins, pathways)
  • Led to many key discoveries
  • FAILS to explain complex emergent phenomena: disease progression, drug resistance, development
  • Example: studying 3-6 genes to understand diabetes — too narrow for complex disease
Systems Biology (Integrated)
  • Studies biological systems at the integrated level
  • Examines ALL components and ALL interactions simultaneously
  • Uses computational and mathematical modeling
  • Reveals emergent properties not visible from parts alone
Definition

Systems Biology is an interdisciplinary field that seeks to understand living organisms at an integrated level — cataloguing all components (genes, proteins, metabolites), measuring their interactions, and using computational models to simulate and predict behavior of the entire system.

🕸️Biological Networks & Parameters

A biological network is a graph where nodes = molecules (proteins, genes, metabolites) and edges = interactions (physical, functional, regulatory). Network analysis extracts biological insight from complex interaction data.

Network TypeNodesEdgesKey Databases
Protein-Protein Interaction (PPI)ProteinsPhysical/functional interactionsSTRING, BioGRID
Gene Regulatory Network (GRN)Genes/TFsTranscriptional regulationTRANSFAC, ENCODE
Metabolic NetworkMetabolites/EnzymesBiochemical reactionsKEGG, Reactome, BiGG
Signal Transduction NetworkSignaling proteinsDirected phosphorylation eventsKEGG PATHWAY, Signor
Node Properties
  • Degree: Number of direct connections
  • Hub: High-degree node — often essential; enriched in disease genes
  • Betweenness Centrality: Fraction of shortest paths passing through a node — bottleneck proteins
  • Clustering Coefficient: How interconnected a node's neighbors are
Network Properties
  • Scale-free: Few hubs, many poorly-connected nodes — power law degree distribution (P(k) ~ k⁻γ)
  • Small world: Most nodes reachable in few steps
  • Modularity: Nodes cluster into functional communities
  • Robustness: Tolerates random failures; vulnerable to hub removal

STRING vs BioGRID

STRING includes predicted interactions (co-expression, text mining, homology) + experimental → broader but lower average confidence. BioGRID focuses on experimentally verified interactions only → more conservative but higher confidence. Use BioGRID as gold-standard training data; STRING for exploratory visualization.
