🛡️Preserving Genomic Integrity

Cellular Defense Triad

1. DNA Repair Pathways (BER, NER, MMR, NHEJ, HR)
2. Cell Cycle Checkpoints (G1/S, intra-S, G2/M, spindle)
3. Apoptosis via p53 when damage is irreparable — together reduce consequences of DNA damage

⚡DNA Damage Response (DDR)

Sensor Kinases
  • ATM: Activated by DSBs via MRN complex (MRE11-RAD50-NBS1) → phosphorylates γH2AX (Ser139), CHK2, BRCA1, p53, MDM2
  • ATR: Activated by ssDNA (stalled forks, resected DSBs) via RPA+ATRIP → phosphorylates CHK1
  • DNA-PKcs: DSB recognition at NHEJ via Ku70/80 → NHEJ coordination
Effectors & Outcomes
  • CHK1/2: Phosphorylate CDC25A/C → CDK inhibition → cell cycle arrest
  • p53 (TP53): Stabilized by CHK2 → activates p21 (G1 arrest) or BAX/PUMA (apoptosis)
  • γH2AX: ATM phosphorylates H2AX Ser139 → visible nuclear foci ≈ 1 DSB/focus → sensitive DSB biomarker
γH2AX as DSB Biomarker

γH2AX foci are used clinically to: (1) measure radiation therapy-induced damage, (2) assess DSB repair kinetics in biopsies, (3) monitor response to chemotherapy, (4) test DDR inhibitors in clinical trials.

NER — Two Subpathways
Global Genome NER (GG-NER)
  • Surveys entire genome for helix-distorting lesions
  • Recognition: XPC-RAD23B detects helix distortion (not the lesion itself)
  • Followed by TFIIH unwinding → XPA verification → dual incision (XPG 3′; XPF-ERCC1 5′) → ~25-30 nt oligonucleotide excised → DNA Pol δ/ε gap fill → Lig I seals
Transcription-Coupled NER (TC-NER)
  • Triggered when RNA Pol II stalls at a lesion on the template strand
  • Recognition: CSB (ERCC6) recognizes stalled Pol II → CSA (ERCC8) complex recruited
  • Faster than GGR for transcribed strands
  • Defects in CSB/CSA → Cockayne Syndrome (premature aging, photosensitivity, neurodegeneration)

💥DSB Repair: NHEJ and Homologous Recombination

NHEJ — Non-Homologous End Joining
  • Active throughout ALL cell cycle phases
  • Error-prone → small indels at cut site → frameshifts → gene KO
  • Proteins: Ku70/80, DNA-PKcs, Artemis (nuclease), XRCC4, DNA Ligase IV, XLF
  • Clinical: used in CRISPR knockout strategy
Homologous Recombination (HR)
  • Active mainly in S/G2 phase (sister chromatid template required)
  • Error-free template-directed repair
  • Key proteins: MRN-CtIP (resection), RPA, BRCA1, BRCA2-RAD51 (strand invasion), RAD54
  • Clinical: BRCA1/2 mutations → HR deficiency → PARP inhibitor sensitivity (synthetic lethality)
🏥 Critical Clinical Connections

Xeroderma Pigmentosum (XP): NER defect (XPA-XPG mutations) → 2000× skin cancer risk. Lynch Syndrome: MMR defect (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2) → MSI-High CRC/endometrial → responds to pembrolizumab (tumor-agnostic FDA approval 2017). BRCA1/2: HR deficiency → breast/ovarian/prostate cancer → PARP inhibitors (olaparib, rucaparib, niraparib) via synthetic lethality. Fanconi Anemia: ICL repair defect (22 FANC genes) → bone marrow failure + cancer predisposition.

📡Signal Transduction Overview

Definition

Signal transduction = process by which cells receive and respond to extracellular signals. Converts chemical/physical signals into intracellular biochemical responses regulating gene expression, metabolism, growth, and survival.

Lecture Outline

🔬Endosymbiotic Theory & Mitochondrial Structure

Mitochondria originated ~1.5-2 billion years ago from engulfment of an aerobic alpha-proteobacterium. Evidence: double membrane, circular mtDNA, binary fission, 55S ribosomes (like bacterial 70S), cardiolipin in IMM, antibiotic sensitivity, phylogenetics.

Mitochondrial Compartments
  • Outer Membrane (OMM): VDAC porins → permeable to ≤5 kDa; fission/fusion proteins (DRP1, MFN1/2, OPA1)
  • Intermembrane Space (IMS): Similar to cytosol for small molecules; cytochrome c trapped here → released in apoptosis
  • Inner Membrane (IMM): Highly impermeable; rich in cardiolipin; cristae folds increase surface area; houses ETC (Complexes I-IV) + ATP Synthase + ANT translocase
  • Matrix: Contains mtDNA, mitoribosomes, TCA cycle enzymes, β-oxidation enzymes
Mitochondrial Genome
  • Circular, dsDNA, 16,569 bp
  • Encodes: 13 proteins (ETC/ATP synthase subunits) + 22 tRNAs + 2 rRNAs
  • Genes lack introns; tightly packed
  • Replication: DNA Pol γ (POLG); transcription factor: TFAM
  • Mutation rate 10-17× higher than nuclear DNA (naked + ROS proximity + limited NER)
  • Maternal inheritance: Sperm mtDNA eliminated post-fertilization via mitophagy

ATP Yield & Boyer's Mechanism

NADH → ~2.5 ATP; FADH₂ → ~1.5 ATP. One glucose → ~30-32 ATP total. Boyer's binding change mechanism (Nobel 1997): H⁺ flow through F₀ c-ring → rotates γ subunit in F₁ → alternating αβ subunit conformations (Open→Loose→Tight) → ATP synthesized. ~2.7 H⁺ per ATP.

🏥 Mitochondrial Replacement Therapy (MRT)

For women with severe mtDNA disease: Spindle Transfer or Pronuclear Transfer — patient's nuclear DNA + healthy donor mitochondria. Result: nuclear DNA of both parents + donor's healthy mtDNA (three-parent baby). First approved in UK (Human Fertilisation and Embryology Act 2015). First clinical birth reported 2016.

Warburg Effect

Cancer cells preferentially use aerobic glycolysis (glucose → lactate) even in the presence of oxygen. Why advantageous: fast ATP, biosynthetic precursors (PPP, serine synthesis), NADPH for antioxidants, acidic microenvironment suppresses immune cells. Exploited clinically by 18F-FDG PET scanning — tumors take up more glucose → hot spots.

AlphaFold Revolution

AlphaFold 2 (DeepMind, Jumper et al., Nature 2021): uses multiple sequence alignments (MSAs) + attention networks (Evoformer architecture) trained on PDB structures → GDT_TS >90 for most proteins. AlphaFold DB: ~200 million protein structures (virtually all UniProt). Nobel Prize Chemistry 2024 (Hassabis & Jumper). Transformed drug discovery, protein engineering, disease mechanism research.

🎯Molecular Docking & Drug Discovery

Molecular docking predicts how a small molecule (ligand) binds to a protein (receptor) — used in drug discovery virtual screening. Two key algorithm types:

Sampling Algorithms
  • Genetic algorithm (most popular — AutoDock/Vina)
  • Monte Carlo algorithm
  • Shape complementarity algorithm
  • Incremental construction algorithm
  • Multiple Copies Simultaneous Search (MCSS)
  • Ludi algorithm
Scoring Algorithms
  • Force field (van der Waals + electrostatics + H-bonds)
  • Empirical scoring (calibrated on known protein-ligand complexes)
  • Knowledge-based (statistical potentials from PDB)
  • Consensus scoring (combination → better accuracy)
Docking Protocol: Step by Step
1
Protein Preparation (PyMOL)

Download PDB structure → remove water (remove resn HOH) → remove existing ligand (remove hetatm) → add hydrogens (h_add) → export as .pdb

2
Ligand Preparation (Chimera/AutoDockTools)

Open .sdf file → assign Gasteiger charges → check protonation states → save as .pdbqt

3
Docking (PyRx/AutoDock Vina)

Load receptor and ligand → make macromolecule → set grid box over binding site → run Vina Wizard → select pose with lowest binding affinity (kcal/mol)

🔬Molecular Dynamics (MD) Simulation

Studies behavior of biomolecules at atomic level over time using Newton's laws applied to every atom. Calculates: U_total = U_bonded + U_non-bonded (force field).

1
System Setup

Solvate protein in TIP3P water box. Add Na⁺/Cl⁻ ions to physiological concentration (0.15 M).

2
Energy Minimization

Remove steric clashes from crystal packing artifacts → minimize total potential energy before simulation.

3
Equilibration

NVT equilibration (target temperature 300K) → NPT equilibration (target pressure 1 atm) → stable conditions before production.

4
Production Run

Run simulation (nanoseconds to microseconds). Atoms move based on computed forces.

5
Analysis

RMSD (Root Mean Square Deviation): overall structural stability — plateau = equilibrated, rising = unfolding. RMSF (Root Mean Square Fluctuation): per-residue flexibility — high RMSF = flexible loop; low RMSF = rigid α-helix. MM-GBSA/MM-PBSA for binding free energy calculation.

Original 6 Hallmarks (Hanahan & Weinberg, 2000)
  1. Sustaining proliferative signaling (RAS/EGFR mutations)
  2. Evading growth suppressors (Rb/p53 loss)
  3. Resisting cell death (BCL2 overexpression)
  4. Enabling replicative immortality (Telomerase activation)
  5. Inducing angiogenesis (VEGF secretion)
  6. Activating invasion & metastasis (EMT, MMPs)
Emerging Hallmarks (2011 & 2022)
  1. Deregulating cellular energetics (Warburg effect)
  2. Avoiding immune destruction (PD-L1, CTLA-4)
  3. Tumor-promoting inflammation (NF-κB, COX-2)
  4. Genome instability & mutation (MMR deficiency)
  5. Unlocking phenotypic plasticity (2022)
  6. Non-mutational epigenetic reprogramming (2022)

Proto-oncogenes → Oncogenes (Gain-of-function, Dominant)
  • Growth factor receptors: EGFR (lung), HER2 (breast), KIT (GIST)

  • Transcription factors: MYC (many cancers), MYCN (neuroblastoma)
  • Cell cycle: Cyclin D1, CDK4 (sarcoma amplification)
Tumor Suppressor Genes (Loss-of-function, Recessive — 2 hits required)
  • Cell cycle control: RB1 (retinoblastoma), CDKN2A/p16
  • DNA damage response: TP53 (>50% all cancers), BRCA1/2, ATM
  • Cell adhesion: APC (FAP; 85% sporadic CRC)
  • PI3K antagonist: PTEN (GBM, endometrial, prostate)
  • MMR: MLH1, MSH2 (Lynch syndrome)

🚀Carcinogenesis, Metastasis & Immune Evasion

1
Initiation

Irreversible carcinogen-induced genetic alteration. Converts proto-oncogenes → oncogenes; inactivates tumor suppressors. STABLE, HERITABLE change. Example: UV-induced TP53 mutation in keratinocyte.

2
Promotion

Clonal expansion of initiated cells by PROMOTERS (non-mutagenic). Promoters: hormones, growth factors, inflammatory mediators, lifestyle factors. Increases proliferation → more mutation opportunities. REVERSIBLE.

3
Progression

Transition benign → malignant. Further genetic/epigenetic changes → genomic instability, invasiveness, metastatic potential, intratumor heterogeneity develops.

EMT (Epithelial-Mesenchymal Transition): ↓ E-cadherin (via SNAIL/ZEB1/2/TWIST TFs) → loss of cell-cell adhesion → gain mesenchymal markers (vimentin, N-cadherin) → motility → MMP-mediated ECM degradation → intravasation → CTCs → extravasation → metastatic colonization.

Angiogenesis: Hypoxia → HIF-1α stabilization → VEGF-A + FGF2 secretion → endothelial proliferation/sprouting → new disorganized, leaky vessels. Anti-VEGF: bevacizumab (CRC, lung, GBM, ovarian).

Immune Evasion: Tumor cells express PD-L1 → binds PD-1 on TILs → SHP-2 phosphatase → dephosphorylates ZAP70 → T cell anergy/exhaustion. Anti-PD-1 (pembrolizumab, nivolumab) or anti-PD-L1 (atezolizumab) → reactivate tumor-infiltrating T cells → tumor killing. Nobel Prize 2018 (Allison + Honjo).

🔬Paradigm Shift: From Bulk to Single Cell

Bulk Sequencing (Classical Approach)
  • Averages signals across thousands/millions of cells → single composite profile
  • Useful for general tissue characteristics
  • MASKS: rare cell populations, cellular heterogeneity, developmental transitions, drug-resistant subclones
  • Example: tumor bulk RNA-Seq reports average expression — misses 2% drug-resistant cancer stem cells
Single Cell Genomics (Modern Approach)
  • Analyzes genome/transcriptome/epigenome of EACH cell individually
  • Reveals cellular heterogeneity within apparently homogeneous populations
  • Identifies rare cell types, lineage trajectories, cell states
  • Applications: cancer heterogeneity, neuroscience, immunology, development, PGT
Key Technical Challenge

Human cell contains only ~6 picograms (10⁻¹² g) of DNA — requires powerful amplification (~100,000×) before sequencing. For scRNA-Seq: reverse transcription + PCR amplification. Amplification bias is a critical technical issue.

💧Drop-Seq: scRNA-Seq Methodology

Massively parallel scRNA-Seq using microfluidics — each nanoliter droplet contains one cell + one barcoded bead.

Barcoded Bead Structure (4 key components, 5′ to 3′)
  • 5′ PCR handle: For downstream library amplification
  • 12-base Cell Barcode: Unique to each bead → identifies cell of origin after pooled sequencing
  • 8-base UMI (Unique Molecular Identifier): Tags individual mRNA molecules → removes PCR duplicates → accurate transcript counting
  • 30-base poly(dT) tail: Captures polyadenylated mRNA from lysed cells by hybridization
1
Cell Suspension Preparation

Enzymatic/mechanical tissue dissociation → single-cell suspension → filter 40 μm mesh → trypan blue viability (>80% required) → dilute to low concentration

2
Droplet Generation

Microfluidic chip: 3 inputs — cells in buffer + barcoded beads in lysis buffer + fluorinated carrier oil + surfactant (Pico-Surf). Controlled flow rates → one cell + one bead per nanoliter droplet (Poisson statistics)

3
Cell Lysis & mRNA Capture

Cells lyse within droplets → polyadenylated mRNA hybridizes to poly(dT) on bead → bead = molecular address for all mRNA from one cell

4
Droplet Breaking & cDNA Synthesis

Perfluorooctanol breaks emulsion → beads collected, washed → Reverse transcription → PCR amplification → Illumina library prep → sequencing

5
Bioinformatic Analysis

Demultiplexing (12-bp barcode) → STAR alignment to reference genome → UMI counting (collapse PCR duplicates) → gene-by-cell count matrix → normalization → PCA → KNN graph → Louvain/Leiden clustering → UMAP visualization → marker gene annotation → cell type assignment

🧬FACS + MDA for Single Cell DNA Sequencing

1
Cell Isolation (FACS)

Flow cytometer sorts individual cells into separate wells based on fluorescent surface markers (CD45+ immune cells, EpCAM+ tumor cells, CD34+ HSCs)

2
Cell Lysis

Alkaline lysis buffer (Proteinase K + detergent + RNase) → releases DNA without fragmentation

3
Whole Genome Amplification (MDA)

Multiple Displacement Amplification: Isothermal 30°C; random hexamer primers; Phi29 DNA polymerase (strand-displacing, highly processive, 3′→5′ proofreading); creates branched DNA amplicons; exponential amplification. Low error rate (~10⁻⁶).

4
Library Prep & Sequencing

Enzymatic/sonication fragmentation → adapter + barcode ligation → PCR → sequencing

5
Bioinformatic Analysis

Demultiplexing → read trimming (Trimmomatic) → QC (FastQC) → alignment (Bowtie2) → SNV calling + CNV analysis per cell
